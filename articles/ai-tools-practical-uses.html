AI Tools and Their Practical Uses

The AI tools landscape is overcrowded with options and undersupplied with honest evaluation. Here's a practical, opinionated guide to the tools that are genuinely useful right now — and what they're actually useful for.

AuthorMarcus T. Ellison
PublishedFebruary 2025
Reading Time~8 minutes
CategoryGuides

The marketing around AI tools has gotten out of hand. Nearly every software product now claims to be "AI-powered." A blender with a smartphone app and some preset programs describes itself as using artificial intelligence. This noise makes it genuinely difficult to identify the tools that are doing something substantially useful — the ones that might actually change how you work, learn, or create.

This guide cuts through the noise. It covers tools that are doing something real, evaluates what they're genuinely good at and where they disappoint, and offers practical guidance on how to use them effectively. The goal isn't comprehensiveness — the landscape changes weekly. It's useful orientation.

Large Language Models: The Foundation

Large language models (LLMs) — the technology behind ChatGPT, Grok, Claude, and Gemini — are the most significant AI development for general users in the past several years. Understanding what they are and what they're actually good for is the foundation for using any of them well.

LLMs are trained on enormous amounts of text and learn to predict plausible continuations. They're remarkably good at text generation, explanation, summarization, translation, code writing, and many forms of reasoning. They're characteristically unreliable for tasks requiring precise factual accuracy, current information, or formal logical verification.

ChatGPT (OpenAI)

The most widely adopted general-purpose LLM interface. Genuinely useful for: drafting and editing text, explaining concepts, writing code, brainstorming, summarizing documents, and general Q&A. The GPT-4-based version handles complex tasks well. Limitations: can be confidently wrong on factual matters, has a knowledge cutoff date, and responses vary in quality in ways that aren't always predictable.

Practical tip: Treat ChatGPT as a very capable first draft generator and thinking partner, not as an authoritative source. Always verify specific factual claims from other sources.

Grok (xAI)

Distinguished by real-time information access through X integration and a less filtered conversational style. Useful for the same general tasks as ChatGPT, with particular strength in tasks involving recent information. Integration with X makes it useful for trend research, news analysis, and understanding current public discourse.

Claude (Anthropic)

Widely regarded as particularly strong for long-document analysis, nuanced writing, and tasks requiring careful reasoning. The context window (the amount of text it can process at once) is large, making it useful for document-intensive work. Many users find it produces particularly balanced, nuanced responses on complex topics.

Gemini (Google)

Google's offering is deeply integrated into Google Workspace, making it the natural choice for users already in that ecosystem. Strong multimodal capabilities. Useful for tasks that benefit from Google integration: working with Gmail, Docs, Sheets, and other Google products with AI assistance.

AI for Writing and Communication

Writing assistance is one of the clearest categories where AI tools deliver genuine, immediate value for a wide range of users.

Drafting: LLMs excel at generating first drafts. The key to using this well is treating the output as raw material, not final copy. Paste a draft from any LLM into your own writing process, rewrite it in your voice, and edit for accuracy. Don't publish LLM output directly — use it to overcome blank-page paralysis and generate structure you can then shape.

Editing: Tools like Grammarly (which now incorporates AI) and LLM-based editing can catch grammatical errors, improve clarity, and flag awkward constructions. These are genuinely useful writing support tools, especially for writers working in a second language or for high-stakes professional communication.

Translation: Google Translate, DeepL, and similar neural translation tools have become remarkably capable. For practical communication across language barriers, they're often sufficient. For nuanced or formal documents, a human translator who uses AI as a tool (rather than relying on it as a final product) is still preferable.

AI for Code and Software Development

This is arguably the area where AI tools currently deliver the highest productivity gains for the right users.

GitHub Copilot is an AI coding assistant that suggests code completions inline as you type. For developers working in supported languages (which includes most major ones), it's become a standard part of the workflow — not because it generates perfect code, but because it accelerates the mechanical parts of coding significantly. Studies suggest productivity gains of 25-50% for certain types of tasks.

LLMs for code are useful for: explaining what a piece of code does, generating boilerplate, debugging by explaining error messages, and writing tests. All of these require human verification — LLM-generated code should be read and understood before being deployed, not treated as automatically correct.

Important: Always Review AI-Generated Code AI coding assistants can generate code that looks correct but contains subtle bugs, security vulnerabilities, or logic errors. Never deploy AI-generated code without understanding what it does and verifying it works as expected.

AI for Research and Information

Perplexity AI is worth specific mention as a tool designed for research — it combines web search with LLM synthesis, providing cited answers that give you sources to follow. It's more transparent than a pure LLM for factual questions because it shows its sources, making verification possible. For research tasks where you need both synthesis and sourcing, it's more useful than ChatGPT alone.

Grokipedia (the platform you're currently on) is designed for structured knowledge building on AI and technology topics specifically. For the use case of genuinely understanding these subjects — not just answering a specific question — the encyclopedic format serves differently than any chatbot or search tool.

Consensus is an AI-powered tool specifically designed for scientific research — it searches peer-reviewed literature and synthesizes findings with citations. For academic and research tasks that require engagement with the primary scientific literature, this is more appropriate than general-purpose LLMs.

AI for Creative Work

Image generation (Midjourney, DALL·E, Stable Diffusion, Adobe Firefly) has matured rapidly and is genuinely useful for concept visualization, ideation, and certain types of design work. The output quality for photo-realistic and illustrative styles has become impressive. Legitimate concerns about training data sourcing and impact on professional illustrators are worth being aware of and weighing thoughtfully.

Music generation (Suno, Udio) can produce surprisingly convincing music in a variety of styles. Current use cases are mainly for background music, demos, and creative exploration rather than final professional output.

How to Evaluate Any AI Tool

Given how fast the landscape moves, frameworks for evaluating new AI tools matter more than specific product recommendations. When you encounter a new AI tool claiming to improve your work, ask:

  • What specific task does it improve, measurably? Vague claims about "enhancing productivity" aren't useful. Specific claims about what it does — and what it costs in time, money, or privacy — are.
  • What are its characteristic failure modes? Every AI tool fails in specific ways. Understanding the failure pattern helps you use the tool in the contexts where it's reliable and verify output appropriately in contexts where it isn't.
  • What does it do with your data? Many free AI tools monetize through data collection. Understand what data the tool has access to, how it's used, and whether you're comfortable with that trade-off.
  • Does it require critical evaluation of its output? The answer for most AI tools is yes. Tools that discourage critical evaluation of their output — that present generated content as simply authoritative — should raise flags.

The most valuable skill in an AI-saturated environment isn't using every AI tool available. It's using a small number of genuinely good tools well, understanding what they're reliable for, and applying critical judgment to their output consistently.
